Quantitative vs. Qualitative & Variables

Quantitative Research: The process of collecting and analyzing numerical data. It finds patterns, tests relationships, and generalizes results.

Qualitative Research: Focuses on narrative (verbal) data to understand human experiences, qualities, and characteristics.

Understanding Variables:

  • Variable: A characteristic that varies from one person to another (e.g., Blood type, Weight).
  • Independent Variable (IV): The presumed cause. The researcher manipulates or observes this to see its effect.
  • Dependent Variable (DV): The presumed effect or outcome.
Example: In a study about "Smoking causing Lung Cancer", Smoking is the Independent Variable, and Lung Cancer is the Dependent Variable.
2

The Research Problem and Hypotheses

Research Problem: A statement that addresses a gap in knowledge, a challenge, or a contradiction.

The PICOT Format:

  • P (Population/Patient): Who is being studied?
  • I (Intervention): What is the treatment or exposure?
  • C (Comparison): What is the alternative or control group?
  • O (Outcome): What is the expected result?
  • T (Time): What is the time frame?

Hypotheses Types:

  • Null Hypothesis (H0): Proposes there is NO relationship between variables.
  • Alternative Hypothesis (H1): States the independent variable DOES affect the dependent variable.
  • Directional Hypothesis: Predicts the specific direction of a relationship (e.g., "increases", "decreases").
3

Finding and Reviewing Literature

Literature Review: A review of the critical points of current knowledge on a topic. It helps prevent duplicating existing work and identifies research gaps.

  • Primary Sources: Original research reports written by the researchers who conducted the study.
  • Secondary Sources: Summaries or analyses of studies done by others.

Database Searching Tools (Boolean Operators):

  • AND: Narrows the search (requires BOTH terms to be present).
  • OR: Broadens the search (requires EITHER term to be present).

CINAHL & MEDLINE: Major databases for finding nursing, medical, and allied health literature.

4

Ethics in Research

Research must be conducted ethically to protect participants' rights.

Core Ethical Principles:

  • Beneficence: Duty to do good.
  • Nonmaleficence: Duty to do no harm.
  • Autonomy: Respecting personal freedom and the right to choose.
  • Informed Consent: Educating a patient/participant about risks and benefits before they participate.

Research Misconduct:

  • Fabrication: Making up data or results.
  • Falsification: Manipulating equipment or changing data.
  • Plagiarism: Using someone else's ideas or words without giving them credit.
5

Sampling Methods

Population: The complete set of people with a common characteristic.

Sample: A selected portion of the population used to represent the entire group.

Types of Sampling:

  • Probability Sampling: Involves random selection. Allows strong statistical inferences. Examples: Simple random sampling, systematic, stratified, cluster.
  • Non-probability Sampling: Involves non-random selection based on convenience. Easier but has higher bias risk. Examples: Convenience sampling, voluntary response, purposive, snowball sampling.
Note: Snowball sampling is used when a population is hard to reach; researchers recruit participants via other participants.
